Adaptive Localization

Self-calibrating hidden-target seeking from cooperative range-bearing beacons.

This ongoing research studies how a robot can localize and approach a target that is not directly observable. Cooperative range-bearing beacons provide indirect measurements, while robot motion supplies the geometric excitation needed to estimate target position, beacon position, and beacon orientation together.

Basic Idea

Motion turns weak cooperative sensing into usable target localization.

A robot receives range-bearing measurements from a friendly beacon or agent, but the beacon pose and yaw are not globally calibrated. From a single static viewpoint, target position, beacon translation, and beacon orientation can share an ambiguity. As the robot moves, distinct relative observations add constraints that reduce this self-calibration ambiguity and make the hidden target observable in simulation.

Estimator Structure

Local-frame sensing couples target localization with beacon self-calibration.

The estimator solves a joint nonlinear problem over the hidden target, beacon positions, and beacon yaw angles. The simulations compare one- and two-beacon configurations, track closed-loop convergence, and test how range and bearing noise affect the recovered geometry.

Observability

Trajectory excitation changes the numerical rank.

A single static vehicle pose leaves the local one-beacon model rank-deficient. Two distinct poses recover full rank for the five unknowns, and the full excited trajectory improves conditioning further.

  • 1 pose: rank 4, singular value 0
  • 2 poses: rank 5, singular value 12.42
  • 80 poses: rank 5, singular value 91.80
Monte Carlo

Noisy trials show low final target error in simulation.

Across the local-frame unknown-yaw scenario, the simulator reports target RMSE near 0.0063 with one beacon and 0.0041 with two beacons under the configured noise level. These are simulation results, not a publication claim.
